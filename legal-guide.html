2. The Dual-License Strategy

The most powerful IP protection strategy for software inventions is dual-licensing:

The Formula

CODE = MIT License (open source)
METHOD = All Rights Reserved (proprietary)

Why This Works

Aspect Code (MIT) Method (Proprietary)
Can others use it? Yes, freely Only with license
Can others copy it? Yes No
Can others sell it? Yes (the code) No (the method)
What's the value? Low (commodity) High (unique)

Example

For MobileCLI:

  • MIT Licensed (Code): HTML, CSS, JavaScript files, setup scripts
  • Proprietary (Method): The combination of Terminal + Claude Code + autonomous sessions + CLAUDE.md knowledge compounding + dual-repo deployment

Anyone can use our code. But the method of combining these technologies for mobile-first AI development? That's the protected invention.

3. Essential Legal Documents

Every project needs these five documents:

3.1 Terms of Service

Governs the relationship between you and users. Must include:

  • Acceptance of Terms
  • License Grant (what users CAN do)
  • Intellectual Property Rights
  • Prohibited Uses
  • Binding Arbitration (Section 12)
  • Class Action Waiver (Section 13)
  • Limitation of Liability
  • Governing Law (Delaware recommended)
  • Severability clause

3.2 Privacy Policy

Required by law if you collect ANY user data:

  • What data you collect
  • How you use it
  • Who you share it with
  • User rights (access, deletion)
  • GDPR compliance (EU users)
  • CCPA compliance (California users)
  • Cookie policy

3.3 Disclaimer

Limits your liability:

  • "As Is" service provision
  • No warranty
  • Limitation of damages
  • Assumption of risk by user
  • Third-party links disclaimer

3.4 IP Notice

Declares what you own:

  • Ownership statement
  • What IS protected (the method)
  • What is NOT protected (third-party tools)
  • Dual-license explanation
  • Statutory damages warning ($150,000)
  • Contact for commercial licensing

3.5 DMCA Policy

Required for safe harbor protection:

  • Designated agent information
  • How to file takedown notice
  • Counter-notification process
  • Repeat infringer policy

4. Invention Documentation

Beyond legal pages, document your invention itself:

GENESIS.md

The origin story and technical specification of your invention:

# GENESIS.md - The Invention

## What We Built
- Date of Invention: [Date]
- Inventor: [Name/Alias]
- Method: [How it was created]

## THE CORE INVENTION (Proprietary IP)

### What IS the Invention:
1. [First novel element]
2. [Second novel element]
3. [The unique combination]

### What is NOT Part of the Invention:
- Third-party tools used
- General techniques available to all
- Existing technologies

### The Novel Contribution:
The COMBINATION and METHOD is the proprietary IP.

IP_NOTICE.md

A clear statement of rights for developers/contributors:

# Intellectual Property Notice

## Ownership
All rights to the METHOD are reserved by [Entity].

## Dual License
- Code: MIT License
- Method: Proprietary - All Rights Reserved

## Contact for Licensing
[email/contact info]

5. Evidence Collection

Documentation proves your invention timeline. Collect:

Digital Evidence

Evidence Type How to Get It Why It Matters
Screenshots Device screenshots with timestamps Shows work in progress
Git commits Regular commits with messages Immutable timestamp trail
GitHub API gh api repos/OWNER/REPO Official creation timestamps
Wayback Machine Submit URLs to archive.org Third-party timestamp proof
Email receipts Service signups, payments Shows when you started

Create a document cataloging all evidence:

# EVIDENCE_TIMELINE.md

## Chronology
| Date | Time | Evidence | Description |
|------|------|----------|-------------|
| Jan 1 | 2:12 PM | Screenshot | First Terminal session |
| Jan 1 | 2:55 PM | Screenshot | First app naming |

## Key Evidence Files

Pro Tip: Archive Early

Submit your URLs to the Wayback Machine immediately after launching. This creates a third-party timestamp that courts recognize as evidence.

curl "https://web.archive.org/save/https://yoursite.com"

6. Third-Party Separation

If your invention uses existing tools, clearly separate what's yours from what's theirs:

Why This Matters

Without clear separation, someone could claim your invention is just "using their tool" and you have no unique contribution. Document the distinction explicitly.

Template

## Third-Party Separation Statement

### Tools Used (NOT our invention):
- Tool A by Creator X - Used for [purpose]
- Tool B by Creator Y - Used for [purpose]

### Our Invention (Proprietary):
- The METHOD of combining these tools
- The specific workflow created
- The novel application
- The resulting platform

### Relationship Status:
- No collaboration occurred
- No exchange of ideas
- Independent invention
- No response/acknowledgment from tool creators

Attribution Requirements by License

Different licenses have different requirements. Here's what you must do:

License Requirements Example
GPLv3 (Termux) Include license & copyright; indicate changes; derivatives must be GPLv3 Copyright © Termux Contributors. Licensed under GPLv3.
Apache 2.0 Include license & copyright; state changes made Copyright © Jack Palevich. Licensed under Apache 2.0.
MIT (Node.js, GitHub CLI) Include copyright and license text Copyright © OpenJS Foundation. MIT License.
Proprietary (Claude Code) Follow vendor's TOS; typically no modification/redistribution © Anthropic PBC. Use subject to Commercial Terms of Service.

Where to Place Attributions

  • IP Notice page - Public acknowledgment of all third-party software
  • Terms of Service - Third-Party Software Acknowledgment section
  • LICENSE file - In your GitHub repository
  • About/Credits page - Optional user-facing acknowledgments

Liability for Third-Party Software

Your Terms must make clear:

MobileDevCLI is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored
by any third-party software providers. Your use of third-party
software is at your own risk and subject to their respective
terms of service. We are not responsible for the functionality,
availability, or security of third-party software.

7. Business Formation

An LLC protects your personal assets and holds your IP:

California LLC Costs

Why Delaware?

Consider forming in Delaware even if you're in California:

  • Business-friendly laws
  • Privacy protection
  • Established case law
  • Used by most tech companies
  • Courts experienced with IP disputes

8. Arbitration & Class Action Waiver

Why Include Arbitration?

  • Faster - Months vs. years in court
  • Cheaper - No full trial costs
  • Private - No public court record
  • Enforceable - Courts uphold arbitration

Sample Clause

Section 12: Binding Arbitration

Any dispute arising from these Terms shall be resolved
through binding arbitration administered by JAMS under
its Comprehensive Arbitration Rules. The arbitration
shall take place in [State], and the arbitrator's
decision shall be final and binding.

Class Action Waiver

Section 13: Class Action Waiver

You agree that any dispute resolution proceedings will
be conducted only on an individual basis and not in a
class, consolidated, or representative action.

Required: Opt-Out Provision

For enforceability, you MUST include a 30-day opt-out period:

You may opt out of this arbitration agreement within
30 days by sending written notice to [address].

9. Complete Checklist

Use this checklist before launching any project:

Legal Documents

  • Terms of Service with arbitration clause
  • Privacy Policy with GDPR/CCPA compliance
  • Disclaimer with liability limitations
  • IP Notice with dual-license explanation
  • DMCA Policy with designated agent

Invention Documentation

  • GENESIS.md - Invention origin and specification
  • IP_NOTICE.md - Rights declaration for developers
  • EVIDENCE_TIMELINE.md - Evidence catalog
  • Third-party separation statement

Evidence Collection

  • Screenshots with timestamps preserved
  • Git commit history established
  • GitHub repo creation timestamps documented
  • Wayback Machine archives submitted
  • Payment/receipt records saved

Website Integration

  • Footer links on ALL pages
  • Legal pages accessible
  • Copyright notice current
  • Contact information provided

Third-Party Compliance

  • GPLv3 attribution (Termux, Git)
  • Apache 2.0 attribution where required
  • MIT license notices (Node.js, GitHub CLI)
  • Proprietary TOS compliance (Claude Code)
  • Third-Party Attributions table in IP Notice
  • Third-Party Software Acknowledgment in Terms

Liability Protection

  • Beta/Development status notice
  • "As Is" disclaimer of warranties
  • Limitation of liability clause
  • Assumption of Risk section
  • Release of Liability language
  • Indemnification clause
  • Device/data responsibility disclaimer
  • Third-party software risk acknowledgment
  • Explicit consent at account creation/payment
